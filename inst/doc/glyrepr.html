Getting Started with glyrepr

Welcome to the world of glycan analysis! If you‚Äôve ever tried to work with glycans computationally, you know the struggle: these tree-like molecules are notoriously difficult to represent and analyze compared to their linear cousins like proteins or DNA. That‚Äôs where glyrepr comes to the rescue.

Think of glyrepr as your glycan translator ‚Äî it teaches your computer how to ‚Äúspeak glycan‚Äù fluently. Whether you‚Äôre dealing with compositions (what‚Äôs in the glycan) or structures (how it‚Äôs connected), this package has got you covered.

library(glyrepr)

Quick Start: What Are We Talking About?

Before we dive in, let‚Äôs establish our vocabulary. Don‚Äôt worry ‚Äî it‚Äôs simpler than it sounds!

Term What It Means Example
Composition The ‚Äúingredients list‚Äù ‚Äî how many of each sugar Hex(5)HexNAc(2)
Structure The ‚Äúblueprint‚Äù ‚Äî how sugars are connected Man(a1-3)Man(b1-4)GlcNAc
Monosaccharide A single sugar unit (the building blocks) Gal, Man, Hex
Linkage The ‚Äúglue‚Äù between sugars a1-3, b1-4
Substitution Chemical decorations on sugars 6Ac, 3Me

üîç Pro tip: We distinguish between generic sugars (like mystery boxes labeled ‚ÄúHex‚Äù) and concrete sugars (like specific boxes labeled ‚ÄúGalactose‚Äù).

Part 1: Compositions ‚Äî The Easy Start

Let‚Äôs start with something straightforward: glycan compositions. Think of these as ingredient lists for your favorite recipes.

Creating Your First Compositions

There are three ways to create compositions, each with its own superpower:

Method 1: The Direct Approach

# Just tell R what you have
glycan_composition(c(Hex = 5, HexNAc = 2), c(Gal = 1, GalNAc = 1))
#> <glycan_composition[2]>
#> [1] Hex(5)HexNAc(2)
#> [2] Gal(1)GalNAc(1)

Method 2: The Programmatic Way

# Perfect when you're processing data from files or databases
comp_list <- list(c(Hex = 5, HexNAc = 2), c(Gal = 1, GalNAc = 1))
as_glycan_composition(comp_list)
#> <glycan_composition[2]>
#> [1] Hex(5)HexNAc(2)
#> [2] Gal(1)GalNAc(1)

Method 3: The Parser

# Copy-paste from your mass spec software? No problem!
as_glycan_composition(c("Hex(5)HexNAc(2)", "H1N1"))
#> <glycan_composition[2]>
#> [1] Hex(5)HexNAc(2)
#> [2] Hex(1)HexNAc(1)

The Magic of Colors üåà

Here‚Äôs something cool: when you run these examples in your R console, you‚Äôll see the concrete monosaccharides (like Gal and GalNAc) displayed in beautiful colors! These follow the SNFG standard ‚Äî the universal ‚Äúcolor code‚Äù for glycans. Think of it as the glycan rainbow üåà.

Smart Counting with count_mono()

Now here‚Äôs where glyrepr shows its intelligence:

comp <- glycan_composition(
  c(Hex = 5, HexNAc = 2),          # generic sugars
  c(Gal = 1, Man = 1, GalNAc = 1)  # concrete sugars
)

# How many galactose residues?
count_mono(comp, "Gal")
#> [1] NA  1

# How many hexose residues? (This includes Gal and Man!)
count_mono(comp, "Hex")
#> [1] 5 2

Notice how count_mono() is smart enough to know that galactose and mannose are both hexoses? That‚Äôs the power of understanding glycan hierarchies!

Part 2: Structures ‚Äî Where the Magic Happens

Compositions are nice, but structures are where glyrepr truly shines. This is like going from knowing the ingredients to understanding the actual recipe and cooking method.

Your First Glycan Structures

Let‚Äôs work with some real glycan structures. These strings below are called the ‚ÄúIUPAC-condensed‚Äù glycan text representations. They might look cryptic, but they‚Äôre actually quite readable once you get the hang of it. To learn about them, check out this article.

iupacs <- c(
  "Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-",  # The famous N-glycan core
  "Gal(b1-3)GalNAc(a1-",                                  # O-glycan core 1
  "Gal(b1-3)[GlcNAc(b1-6)]GalNAc(a1-",                    # O-glycan core 2
  "Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-",      # A branched mannose tree
  "GlcNAc6Ac(b1-4)Glc3Me(a1-"                             # With some decorations
)

struc <- as_glycan_structure(iupacs)
struc
#> <glycan_structure[5]>
#> [1] Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-
#> [2] Gal(b1-3)GalNAc(a1-
#> [3] Gal(b1-3)[GlcNAc(b1-6)]GalNAc(a1-
#> [4] Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-
#> [5] GlcNAc6Ac(b1-4)Glc3Me(a1-
#> # Unique structures: 5

The Secret Sauce: Unique Structure Optimization

Here‚Äôs where glyrepr gets really clever. Notice that ‚Äú# Unique structures: 5‚Äù message? This isn‚Äôt just informational ‚Äî it‚Äôs the key to lightning-fast performance.

Let‚Äôs see this optimization in action:

# Create a big dataset with lots of repetition
large_struc <- rep(struc, 1000)  # 5,000 structures total
large_struc
#> <glycan_structure[5000]>
#> [1] Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-
#> [2] Gal(b1-3)GalNAc(a1-
#> [3] Gal(b1-3)[GlcNAc(b1-6)]GalNAc(a1-
#> [4] Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-
#> [5] GlcNAc6Ac(b1-4)Glc3Me(a1-
#> [6] Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-4)GlcNAc(b1-
#> [7] Gal(b1-3)GalNAc(a1-
#> [8] Gal(b1-3)[GlcNAc(b1-6)]GalNAc(a1-
#> [9] Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-3)[Man(a1-6)]Man(a1-
#> [10] GlcNAc6Ac(b1-4)Glc3Me(a1-
#> ... (4990 more not shown)
#> # Unique structures: 5

Still showing ‚Äú# Unique structures: 5‚Äù! This means glyrepr is storing only 5 unique graphs internally, not 5,000. This is like having a smart library system that stores only one copy of each book, no matter how many people want to read it.

Performance That Will Blow Your Mind üöÄ

Let‚Äôs put this to the test:

library(tictoc)

tic("Converting 5 structures")
result_small <- convert_to_generic(struc)
toc()
#> Converting 5 structures: 0.008 sec elapsed

tic("Converting 5,000 structures")

toc()
#> Converting 5,000 structures: 0.01 sec elapsed

Mind = blown! ü§Ø The performance is nearly identical because glyrepr only processes each unique structure once, then cleverly expands the results.

Understanding Structure Resolution Levels üî¨

Not all glycan structures are created equal ‚Äî they come in different levels of detail, like zoom levels on a map. glyrepr recognizes four resolution levels:

  • ‚Äúintact‚Äù: The full picture ‚Äî all monosaccharides are concrete (e.g., ‚ÄúMan‚Äù, ‚ÄúGlcNAc‚Äù), and all linkages are fully determined (e.g., ‚Äúa2-3‚Äù, ‚Äúb1-4‚Äù).
  • ‚Äúpartial‚Äù: Almost there ‚Äî all monosaccharides are concrete (e.g., ‚ÄúMan‚Äù, ‚ÄúGlcNAc‚Äù), but some linkage information is missing (e.g., ‚Äúa2-?‚Äù).
  • ‚Äútopological‚Äù: We know what‚Äôs there, but not how they connect ‚Äî all monosaccharides are concrete (e.g., ‚ÄúMan‚Äù, ‚ÄúGlcNAc‚Äù), but the linkage information is completely unknown (‚Äú??-?‚Äù).
  • ‚Äúbasic‚Äù: The minimalist view ‚Äî all monosaccharides are generic (e.g., ‚ÄúHex‚Äù, ‚ÄúHexNAc‚Äù), and the linkage information is completely unknown (‚Äú??-?‚Äù).

üí° Fun fact: In theory, you could have a glycan with generic monosaccharides but fully determined linkages (e.g., ‚ÄúHex(b1-3)HexNAc(a1-‚Äù)). But in practice, this is almost unheard of ‚Äî linkage information is much harder to obtain than monosaccharide information. That‚Äôs why glyrepr assigns these structures to the ‚Äúbasic‚Äù level too.

You can get the structure level for a glycan structure vector with get_structure_level():

glycans <- as_glycan_structure(c(
  "Gal(b1-3)GalNAc(a1-",
  "Gal(b1-?)GalNAc(a1-",
  "Gal(??-?)GalNAc(??-",
  "Hex(??-?)HexNAc(??-",
  "Hex(b1-3)HexNAc(a1-"
))
get_structure_level(glycans)
#> [1] "intact"      "partial"     "topological" "basic"       "basic"

Structure Manipulation Tools

glyrepr comes with several handy tools for structure manipulation:

Strip away the connections:

remove_linkages(struc)
#> <glycan_structure[5]>
#> [1] Man(??-?)[Man(??-?)]Man(??-?)GlcNAc(??-?)GlcNAc(??-
#> [2] Gal(??-?)GalNAc(??-
#> [3] GlcNAc(??-?)[Gal(??-?)]GalNAc(??-
#> [4] Man(??-?)[Man(??-?)]Man(??-?)[Man(??-?)]Man(??-
#> [5] GlcNAc6Ac(??-?)Glc3Me(??-
#> # Unique structures: 5

Remove the decorations:

# Let's look at our decorated structure first
struc[5]
#> <glycan_structure[1]>
#> [1] GlcNAc6Ac(b1-4)Glc3Me(a1-
#> # Unique structures: 1

# Now remove the decorations (6Ac and 3Me)
remove_substituents(struc[5])
#> <glycan_structure[1]>
#> [1] GlcNAc(b1-4)Glc(a1-
#> # Unique structures: 1

  mutate(n_man = count_mono(structures, "Man")) %>%
